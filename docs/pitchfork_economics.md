# Pitchfork Economics

## Purpose

This document defines the economic philosophy and risk model for Pitchfork.

The broader product is a life-powered gathering and crafting RPG. This document focuses only on the economic layer: tokens, capped rewards, crypto analogies, symbolic basic income, farming risks, verification, household stewardship, cooperative pools, real-money boundaries, and social harms.

The central economic question is:

> How can Pitchfork recognize real human care, rhythm, and contribution without turning life into extractable behavioral labor?

## Core Economic Thesis

Pitchfork should not begin as a real-money earning app.

The first economy should be:

- Symbolic.
- Internal.
- Capped.
- Non-speculative.
- Privacy-preserving.
- Resistant to farming.
- Built around crafting and contribution rather than raw behavior extraction.

The long-term ambition may include real economic support, grants, cooperative treasuries, patronage, or crypto rails. But those should come only after the symbolic economy proves healthy.

## What Pitchfork Should Avoid

Pitchfork must not become:

- "Do chores, earn money."
- "Track more behavior, extract more value."
- "Optimize your body for token yield."
- "Turn caregiving into gig labor."
- "Speculate on health data."
- "Financialize every intimate action."
- "Punish low-capacity users for resting."
- "Create social pressure around streaks and output."

The product should reward participation and meaning, not maximum productivity.

## Economic Layers

Pitchfork can have several layers of value. These should be introduced gradually.

### 1. Raw Materials

Raw materials are symbolic resources generated by real-world rituals.

Examples:

- Ember Moss from movement.
- Lucid Dew from rest.
- Order Salt from household stewardship.
- Rune Fragments from study.
- Hearthlight from care.
- Memory Ink from reflection.

Raw materials should be capped and mostly non-tradable in early versions. They represent participation, not labor hours.

### 2. Essences

Essences are refined resources created from materials or earned through meaningful patterns.

Examples:

- Fire Essence.
- Dream Essence.
- Hearth Essence.
- Study Essence.
- Care Essence.
- Gold Essence.

Essences are closer to tokens. They can be used for crafting, covenants, upgrades, and later economic systems.

### 3. Crafted Items

Crafted items are higher-order objects made from materials and essences.

Examples:

- Sleep Draught.
- Threshold Ward.
- Study Sigil.
- Vitality Charm.
- Caretaker's Lantern.
- Ledger Stone.
- Hearth Bowl.

Crafted items are healthier economic objects than raw behavioral rewards because their value comes from recipe choice, symbolic interpretation, scarcity, taste, and use, not direct extraction of tracked activity.

### 4. Soulbound Achievements

Some value should never be tradable.

Soulbound achievements may include:

- Personal milestones.
- Recovery arcs.
- Archetype evolution.
- Mentor trust.
- Covenant reputation.
- Major sanctuary restorations.
- Caregiving arcs.
- Household stewardship progress.

This preserves meaning and prevents total financialization of identity.

### 5. Covenants

Covenants are structured commitments.

They can model:

- Smart contracts.
- Escrow.
- Settlement.
- Reputation.
- Witnessing.
- Staking.
- Conditional reward.

In early versions, covenants should be symbolic and single-player. In later versions, they can coordinate guilds, households, mutual aid, or community projects.

### 6. Cooperative Pools

Cooperative pools are shared treasuries or resource stores.

Possible uses:

- Mutual aid.
- Emergency funds.
- Escape grants.
- Childcare support.
- Household support.
- Education support.
- Community projects.
- Guild infrastructure.

These are the most promising route for real social value because they support people through cooperative structures rather than paying them per tracked action.

## Crypto Concept Mapping

Pitchfork can teach crypto concepts through fantasy-native economic systems.

| Technical Concept | In-World Concept | Product Role |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Wallet | Soul sigil | Player identity and inventory boundary |
| Private key | True name | Control and identity risk metaphor |
| Token | Essence, shard, sigil | Portable unit of value |
| NFT | Soulbound relic or crafted artifact | Unique item or achievement |
| Smart contract | Binding covenant | Conditional agreement |
| Escrow | Sealed ritual | Locked value pending settlement |
| Staking | Mana attunement | Committed value or reputation |
| Validator | Rune keeper | Trusted or protocol-level witness |
| Oracle | Omen, witness, scrying mirror | Source of external truth |
| Consensus | Ritual synchronization | Agreement on state |
| Ledger | Codex, Nexus record | History of outcomes |
| DAO | Mage council, guild order | Collective governance |
| Marketplace | Bazaar, trade hall | Exchange of crafted value |
| Treasury | Guild vault | Shared resources |

The early product should not expose these technical terms. The mapping exists for design consistency and later educational reveal.

## Participation-Based Rewards

The safest economic base is capped participation.

The system should reward showing up, not maximizing output.

Examples:

- Maximum 3 ritual completions that produce full rewards per day.
- Maximum 1 material bundle per domain per day.
- Weekly ritual bonuses instead of infinite daily farming.
- Seasonal participation rewards.
- Soft caps that convert extra activity into flavor, story, or reflection rather than more economic value.

Without caps:

```text
more optimization -> more yield -> more pressure -> more extraction
```

With caps:

```text
basic participation -> baseline reward access -> reduced pressure
```

The cap is not just a balance mechanic. It is an ethical commitment.

## Symbolic Basic Income

Pitchfork can explore a symbolic basic income model.

The idea:

- Players receive a modest baseline of participation value per cycle.
- They do not need to grind endlessly.
- Rest, care, study, household maintenance, and other low-visibility work can all qualify.
- The system recognizes presence and contribution without demanding proof of maximum productivity.

In-game, this might look like:

- Daily sanctuary stipend.
- Weekly guild allotment.
- Lunar cycle essence distribution.
- Seasonal harvest share.
- Care pool recognition.

If this ever becomes real economic support, it should be framed as participation-based support or cooperative distribution, not payment for every measured behavior.

## Farming and Exploit Risks

Any reward system will be farmed.

Pitchfork should assume:

- Players may spam easy rituals.
- Players may self-attest falsely.
- Players may create trivial covenants.
- Players may optimize for yield instead of meaning.
- Groups may collude if trading exists.
- Bots may attempt to generate rewards if real value exists.

### Anti-Farming Tools

Use:

- Daily caps.
- Weekly caps.
- Domain caps.
- Diminishing returns.
- Recipe bottlenecks.
- Soulbound rewards.
- Non-tradable raw participation materials.
- Cooldowns.
- Randomized but bounded gathering tables.
- Attunement diversity bonuses.
- Manual review only for high-stakes cooperative pools.
- Clear separation between symbolic rewards and real-money rewards.

Avoid:

- Infinite per-action rewards.
- Direct conversion from steps/tasks to money.
- Public leaderboards based on raw output.
- High-value rewards for sensitive health or household data.

## Verification Philosophy

Pitchfork should be symbolic, not forensic.

Early verification should be self-attested:

- "I did this."
- "I rested."
- "I cleaned."
- "I studied."
- "I cared for someone."

That is acceptable because the app begins as a personal ritual system.

The system should not try to police sincerity aggressively. If someone uses the ritual structure to reflect and participate, that may be valuable even when the action is not externally verified.

The better question is often:

> Did this person show up and participate in the symbolic order?

not:

> Can the platform prove this behavior through invasive data capture?

### Optional Verification

Later, optional verification may include:

- Step count.
- Sleep import.
- Calendar sessions.
- Timer sessions.
- Manual journal entries.
- Photo proof, only for non-sensitive uses.
- Witness signatures.

These should be opt-in and domain-specific.

## The Oracle Problem

If Pitchfork adds covenants and real rewards, it inherits the oracle problem:

> How does the system know whether an outside-world action really happened?

Possible oracle types:

- Self-attestation.
- Device data.
- Calendar data.
- Trusted witness.
- Group confirmation.
- Manual moderator.
- No verification, only symbolic settlement.

Each oracle has tradeoffs:

- Device data can be convenient but invasive.
- Self-attestation is humane but gameable.
- Witnesses add trust but can create social pressure.
- Moderation does not scale.
- Automated signals can encode bias.

For most personal play, self-attestation is the correct default.

## Household Stewardship and Care Work

One of Pitchfork's deeper economic missions is recognizing stewardship.

The risky framing is:

> "Pay people, especially women, to run households."

The stronger framing is:

> "People who perform essential stewardship work can receive recognition, status, resources, and economic support."

This includes:

- Mothers.
- Fathers.
- Caregivers.
- Homemakers.
- Disabled people.
- Elders.
- Community organizers.
- Older siblings.
- Mutual aid workers.
- Teachers.
- Household managers.

The product can still be attentive to the fact that women disproportionately perform unpaid household and caregiving labor. But the design must avoid reinforcing gender roles or implying that care work belongs to one group.

## Household Labor as Economic Infrastructure

Modern economies often undercount:

- Cleaning.
- Cooking.
- Childcare.
- Scheduling.
- Emotional support.
- Grocery planning.
- Laundry.
- Restocking.
- Budgeting.
- Maintaining relationships.
- Caring for elders.
- Helping children learn.

Pitchfork's economic model can represent this as infrastructure work.

In-world examples:

| Household Action | Economic Interpretation | In-World Form |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Cleaning | Maintenance and entropy reduction | Sanctuary stabilization |
| Cooking | Nourishment production | Hearth alchemy |
| Budgeting | Resource allocation | Ledger balancing |
| Childcare | Human development | Lineage flame tending |
| Scheduling | Coordination | Time-thread weaving |
| Emotional support | Social resilience | Sanctuary morale |
| Laundry | Restoration of usable goods | Purification ritual |
| Grocery planning | Supply chain management | Household provisioning |
| Restocking | Inventory management | Vault fortification |

The goal is not to gamify domestic obligation. The goal is to make care visible, prestigious, and supportable.

## Behavioral Optimization Risks

Pitchfork must be careful not to create a new optimization regime.

Risks:

- Players may feel every action must be logged.
- Rest may become instrumentalized.
- Care may become performative.
- Household members may pressure each other to produce rewards.
- Sensitive health patterns may become economically valuable.
- Users may compare outputs and feel inferior.
- Streaks may create shame.
- The game may reward people with more free time and punish people under stress.

Mitigations:

- Caps.
- Rest days.
- Private-by-default logs.
- No raw-output leaderboards.
- Gentle streak design.
- Multiple valid paths.
- Recovery mechanics.
- Low-stakes baseline rewards.
- Ability to pause without punishment.
- No requirement to log sensitive domains.

## Social Harms to Avoid

### Surveillance Capitalism

The system should not monetize intimate behavioral data. Sensitive logs should not become ad-targeting inputs, risk scores, or financial profiles.

### Domestic Extraction

The product should not convert household labor into a new platform-managed gig economy.

### Gender Role Reinforcement

The product should recognize care without implying that women, mothers, or specific groups are naturally responsible for it.

### Health Data Speculation

Health, sleep, fertility, cycle, and mental health data must not become tradable yield signals.

### Coercive Guilds or Households

Future multiplayer systems could create pressure: "Log more so our guild earns more." The design needs opt-outs, privacy boundaries, and caps.

### Financialization of Identity

Not every achievement should be tradable. Soulbound identity protects meaning.

### Punishment Spirals

Users who miss days should be offered recovery, not escalating penalties.

## Privacy Principles

Default privacy posture:

- Manual entry is always allowed.
- Sensitive domains are opt-in.
- Local-first storage should be considered where possible.
- Public sharing is off by default.
- Economic data and personal ritual data should be separable.
- Users can hide, archive, or delete personal logs.
- Aggregation should avoid exposing household, health, fertility, or location patterns.

High-sensitivity data:

- Menstrual and fertility data.
- Mental health notes.
- Location.
- Household composition.
- Caregiving patterns.
- Financial details.
- Child-related data.

These should not be needed for the MVP.

## Trade and Market Design

If trading is introduced, it should begin with crafted items, not raw behavioral yield.

Better:

```text
capped participation -> materials -> recipes -> crafted items -> optional trade
```

Worse:

```text
tracked behavior -> token yield -> cash market
```

Early market rules:

- Raw materials are non-tradable or heavily limited.
- Crafted items can be traded in small quantities.
- Soulbound achievements cannot be traded.
- No real money.
- No real crypto.
- No external speculation.
- NPC markets before player markets.

## Real Economic Layer

Real economic support should come late, if at all.

Possible future forms:

- Stablecoin grants.
- Cooperative treasury distributions.
- Creator marketplace.
- Patronage.
- Community grants.
- Household stewardship funds.
- Mutual aid pools.
- Guild-funded support.

Requirements before real value:

- Proven symbolic economy.
- Strong anti-farming controls.
- Privacy architecture.
- Abuse mitigation.
- Legal review.
- Tax and compliance review.
- Clear separation between play rewards and financial support.
- No sensitive behavioral data as yield basis.

The real economic layer should support people without turning their lives into extractable labor.

## UBI and Cooperative Treasury Model

If Pitchfork experiments with UBI-like systems, the healthiest model is likely cooperative and capped.

Possible model:

1. A guild, community, sponsor, or patron funds a treasury.
2. Eligible participants receive baseline symbolic or real support.
3. Participation confirms presence, not maximum productivity.
4. Caps prevent competitive grinding.
5. Additional crafted contributions can create status or in-world value, but not unlimited payout.

This supports:

- Stability.
- Dignity.
- Mutual aid.
- Low-pressure participation.
- Recognition of care.

It avoids:

- Gigification.
- Surveillance.
- Winner-take-most farming.
- Health-data extraction.

## Governance

Economic systems need governance before they need scale.

Governance questions:

- Who sets reward caps?
- Who funds cooperative pools?
- Who decides eligibility?
- How are disputes handled?
- Can guilds exploit members?
- Can users appeal moderation?
- What data can councils see?
- How are treasury allocations audited?
- What remains private no matter what?

In-world governance can be represented as:

- Mage councils.
- Guild orders.
- Rune keepers.
- Covenant witnesses.
- Sanctuary stewards.

But the real design must answer the human questions underneath the fantasy.

## Recommended Economic Rollout

### Phase 1: Symbolic Single-Player Economy

Include:

- Capped materials.
- Crafting.
- Soulbound achievements.
- Sanctuary upgrades.
- No trade.
- No real money.

Goal:

> Prove that symbolic value feels meaningful.

### Phase 2: Covenants and Internal Ledger

Include:

- Sealed rituals.
- Escrow-like mechanics.
- Settlement records.
- Reputation.
- No external value.

Goal:

> Teach contract logic safely.

### Phase 3: Simulated Markets

Include:

- NPC trade.
- Crafted item exchange.
- Fictional prices.
- Guild vault prototypes.

Goal:

> Study economic behavior without real stakes.

### Phase 4: Cooperative Pools

Include:

- Shared treasuries.
- Mutual aid mechanics.
- Contribution records.
- Strong privacy controls.

Goal:

> Test social support structures.

### Phase 5: Real Economic Experiments

Only if earlier phases are healthy.

Include:

- Grants.
- Stablecoin or fiat payouts.
- Patronage.
- Cooperative treasury distributions.
- Legal and compliance review.

Goal:

> Provide support without extraction.

## Bottom Line

Pitchfork's economy should recognize invisible value without consuming it.

The safest path is:

```text
symbolic participation
-> capped materials
-> crafting
-> soulbound meaning
-> covenants
-> cooperative pools
-> carefully governed real support, maybe
```

The dangerous path is:

```text
behavior tracking
-> uncapped yield
-> speculation
-> pressure
-> surveillance
-> extraction
```

The economic design should keep returning to one principle:

> Reward presence and care without turning life into a mine.
