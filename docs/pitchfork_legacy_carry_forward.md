# Pitchfork Legacy Carry-Forward

## Purpose

The old Pitchfork and Mystical Mastery docs contain useful mechanics and imagery, but they were written before the current social-risk model was explicit.

This document records which legacy ideas should survive and how they should be interpreted in the current direction.

## Current Canon

The current direction is defined by:

- [Pitchfork Ecosystem](pitchfork_ecosystem.md)
- [Pitchfork Economics](pitchfork_economics.md)
- [Pitchfork RPG Client](pitchfork_rpg_client.md)

Everything in [pitchfork-drafts](pitchfork-drafts/) is archived source material, not current canon.

## Useful Legacy Ideas

### Color Magic

The older six-color magic model remains useful as visual language and spell flavor.

Carry forward:

- Colored domains as a readable magical interface.
- Color combinations for recipes, rituals, and crafted items.
- Color as flavor, not as a hard ranking of people or life activities.

Adjust:

- Avoid treating colors as productivity categories.
- Prefer the newer attunement model when representing a player's life pattern.

### Token Rarity

The old common, rare, and legendary token tiers remain useful for game feel.

Carry forward:

- Rarity as item presentation.
- Rarity as recipe complexity.
- Rarity as a pacing and unlock tool.

Adjust:

- Do not use rarity as a direct valuation of human worth.
- Do not let rare/legendary outcomes create pressure to over-log, overwork, or optimize sensitive life domains.

### Contracts

The old contract model remains central to Pitchfork's accounting imagination.

Carry forward:

- Commitments.
- Escrow-like locked value.
- Witnessing.
- Settlement.
- Reputation.
- Shared goals.

Adjust:

- In the RPG client, introduce contracts late as covenants or sealed rituals.
- Do not make contracts the first emotional hook.
- Avoid productivity-contract framing for ordinary life.

### Commodities and Markets

The old commodity conversion system remains useful as a late-game metaphor for resource transformation and market literacy.

Carry forward:

- Materials and essences can transform through recipes, markets, or ritual exchange.
- Commodity imagery can add texture to magic systems.
- Market-like systems can teach exchange, scarcity, and coordination.

Adjust:

- Start with symbolic, internal, capped economies.
- Prefer crafted-item exchange over raw behavior-to-token markets.
- Keep real money and real crypto out of early versions.
- Do not let markets determine a universal cultural ranking of human activities.

### Mentors

The old mentor list remains useful, but the role changes.

Carry forward:

- Mentors as domain guides.
- Mentors as sources of recipes, interpretation, lore, and witness.
- Existing mentor archetypes where they fit the newer domains.

Adjust:

- Mentors should not primarily be task dispensers.
- Mentors should not become managers of player productivity.

### Inventory and UI

The old inventory ideas remain useful for the RPG client.

Carry forward:

- Visible materials, essences, crafted items, covenants, and commodities.
- Distinct sections for resources, commitments, and crafting.
- Token shape, color, and rarity as tactile UI affordances.

Adjust:

- The first UI should support the MVP loop: log activity, gather material, craft, restore, unlock.
- Wallets, markets, and contract-heavy screens should come after the core loop works.

### Lore

The old parallel-world lore remains useful as backstory.

Carry forward:

- A magical world and a commerce/contract world influencing each other.
- Magical interpretation of ordinary activity.
- Ethical tension around power, contracts, and exchange.

Adjust:

- The new emotional center is not commerce.
- The central fantasy is life activity becoming magical presence, care, restoration, and contribution.

## Ideas to Treat as Superseded

The following older framings should not guide implementation without redesign:

- Direct task-to-token-to-cash mechanics.
- Real-money earning as an early product promise.
- Markets as the main way to determine the value of ordinary life.
- Public leaderboards based on raw output.
- Health, sleep, fertility, household, or care data as tradable yield signals.
- Infinite per-action rewards.
- A universal score that ranks people by life activity.
