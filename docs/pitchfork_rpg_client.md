# Pitchfork RPG Client Product Concept

## Product Summary

The Pitchfork RPG client should begin as a single-player, text-heavy, browser-based life-powered gathering and crafting RPG.

Pitchfork itself is the accounting, token, contract, ledger, and eventual crypto layer. Pancakes is the broader human-facing life system. The Pitchfork RPG client is one alternate interface to that accounting layer, more tightly coupled to the magical mechanics, lore, crafting, and symbolic economy.

The player does not simply manage habits. The player synchronizes ordinary life with a symbolic magical world. Walking, sleeping, studying, cooking, cleaning, resting, journaling, exercising, organizing, caregiving, and creating become forms of magical participation.

This client should be inspired by:

- **Kingdom of Loathing:** low-friction browser RPG structure, daily actions, quirky writing, simple screens, inventory, crafting, and location-based play.
- **Habitica:** real-life habits and routines power progress.
- **Cozy crafting games:** gathering, recipes, sanctuary improvement, collections, and long-term cultivation.
- **Pitchfork's crypto layer:** ownership, tokens, contracts, staking, escrow, ledgers, and shared economies are introduced as magical systems over time.

The immediate goal is not to build a crypto platform, MMO, or complete habit tracker. The immediate goal is to prove one emotional loop:

```text
I did something meaningful in real life.
The game interpreted it mythically.
I gathered a magical resource.
I used that resource to craft, restore, or unlock something.
My life felt connected to a world.
```

## One-Sentence Pitch

The Pitchfork RPG client is a life-powered fantasy RPG where real-world care, rhythm, learning, movement, rest, and stewardship become magical materials used to craft relics, restore sanctuaries, deepen identity, and eventually participate in a shared symbolic economy.

## Collaborator-Facing Summary

The Pitchfork RPG client is a game about making invisible forms of human value visible. Many games reward conquest, extraction, accumulation, and combat. This client rewards cultivation: walking, sleeping, cooking, studying, maintaining a household, caring for others, reflecting, organizing, resting, and creating.

The first RPG client is intentionally modest: a single-player browser RPG with text, inventory, recipes, daily rhythm, and sanctuary progression. But the larger design direction is broad. Pitchfork can support a multi-client ecosystem where different real-life domains become portals into the same accounting layer, shared magical world, and eventually into a shared MMO-like civilization animated by ordinary human care.

The crypto and economics layer is not the starting point for the player. It is the deeper substrate. Ownership, contracts, escrows, ledgers, guild treasuries, capped participation rewards, and cooperative pools should emerge as magical systems only after the core gathering and crafting loop feels meaningful.

## Core Promise

The Pitchfork RPG client turns lived activity into magical presence.

The product should make the player feel:

> "My way of caring for life matters, and the world can see it."

The player is not being optimized. They are not being judged by productivity metrics. They are cultivating a magical identity through rhythm, care, consistency, rest, craft, and contribution.

## Product Positioning

The Pitchfork RPG client is not:

- A generic todo list.
- A productivity dashboard with fantasy decoration.
- A fitness tracker with XP.
- A crypto wallet tutorial.
- A domestic labor marketplace.
- A system for surveillance or behavioral wage labor.

The Pitchfork RPG client is:

- A life-powered RPG.
- A gathering and crafting game.
- A symbolic economy of care, rhythm, and stewardship.
- A fantasy interface for real-world activity.
- An interface to the long-term Pitchfork foundation for contracts, guilds, trade, and cooperative economies.

The first product should work even if the player never thinks about crypto. The crypto-inspired systems should appear first as fantasy mechanics: soul sigils, essence, relics, covenants, sealed rituals, ledgers, rune keepers, and guild vaults.

## Audience

Primary audiences:

- Players who enjoy low-friction browser RPGs, text adventures, inventory systems, and crafting.
- People who want a gentler alternative to productivity apps.
- Habitica-like users who want more worldbuilding, less gamified punishment, and richer mechanics.
- Cozy game players interested in ritual, care, restoration, and slow progression.
- Collaborators interested in games that model economics, care, and coordination through metaphor.

Secondary audiences:

- Crypto-curious users who dislike jargon but may understand systems through play.
- Educators and designers interested in covert learning.
- Mutual aid, household, care, and community organizers interested in symbolic economies.
- Future MMO players who want non-combat forms of meaningful presence.

## Design Pillars

### Life Becomes Material

The central mechanic is symbolic transformation. Real-world activity does not become XP directly. It becomes a resource with character, texture, and meaning.

### Crafting Creates Meaning

The payoff is not only earning. The payoff is making: potions, charms, wards, rooms, sigils, relics, rituals, and eventually civic infrastructure.

### Rest Is Valid Play

The system must treat rest, recovery, and low-energy participation as real contributions. Pitchfork should not become a machine for self-optimization.

### Care Is Prestigious

Household maintenance, caregiving, emotional labor, preparation, cleaning, cooking, and planning should feel important in-world. The game should glorify cultivation as much as other games glorify conquest.

### Economics Emerges From Trust

The Commerce World should grow naturally out of crafting, covenants, reputation, and shared contribution. Markets and contracts should feel like magical coordination systems, not financial dashboards.

## The Three Worlds

Pitchfork is built around three connected worlds.

### 1. The Real World

This is the player's actual life.

Real-world actions include:

- Walking.
- Sleeping.
- Exercising.
- Eating.
- Cooking.
- Cleaning.
- Studying.
- Journaling.
- Meditating.
- Caregiving.
- Organizing a household.
- Planning.
- Resting.
- Creating.
- Maintaining relationships.

These actions should not be treated as raw productivity inputs. They are forms of rhythm, stewardship, ritual participation, and life-energy.

### 2. The Magic World

This is the RPG layer the player experiences.

The player sees:

- Gathering.
- Crafting.
- Sanctuaries.
- Rituals.
- Attunements.
- Archetypes.
- Relics.
- Magical regions.
- Mentors.
- Guilds and covenants, later.
- Magical ecology.

The Magic World is the symbolic interpretation of lived reality.

The app should avoid language like:

```text
You synced 8,000 steps and gained 80 XP.
```

It should prefer language like:

```text
Your morning walk gathered ember moss from the ley road.
```

or:

```text
Your rest restored lucid dew in the dream garden.
```

or:

```text
Your household ritual reduced entropy in your sanctuary.
```

### 3. The Commerce World

This is the economic, contractual, and coordination layer.

It contains the crypto-inspired concepts, expressed in-world:

| Technical Concept | In-World Concept |
| --- | --- |
| Wallet | Soul sigil |
| Private key | True name |
| Token | Essence, shard, sigil, relic |
| Smart contract | Binding covenant |
| Staking | Mana attunement |
| Escrow | Sealed ritual |
| Validator | Rune keeper |
| Consensus | Ritual synchronization |
| Marketplace | Bazaar, trade hall, astral exchange |
| NFT | Soulbound relic or crafted artifact |
| Reputation | Renown, trust, lineage |
| DAO | Mage council, guild, covenant order |

The Commerce World should not be present in full at launch. It should emerge gradually after the player understands gathering, crafting, inventory, and sanctuary progression.

## Core Design Principle

Pitchfork is not a productivity app with fantasy decoration.

It is an RPG powered by reality.

A weak version says:

> "Do chores to earn points."

A strong version says:

> "Your life patterns generate magical resources, shape your identity, and contribute to a civilization."

The core loop is:

```text
Real-world action
-> symbolic interpretation
-> magical resource generation
-> gathering / crafting / ritual use
-> identity progression
-> sanctuary or world contribution
-> renewed motivation for real-world action
```

The system should feel more like cultivation than conquest.

## Core Product Loop

The first RPG client loop should be:

1. The player opens their Sanctuary dashboard.
2. They choose or review daily rituals.
3. They log real-world activities.
4. The system interprets those activities as gathering.
5. The player receives magical materials.
6. The player crafts items, brews potions, restores rooms, or performs rituals.
7. The player's archetype, attunements, sanctuary, and story progress.
8. New recipes, regions, mentors, and systems unlock.

The first loop is not:

```text
Task -> XP -> level.
```

It is:

```text
Life activity -> magical material -> crafted meaning -> world change.
```

## Initial Player Experience

The first session should take less than five minutes.

Suggested onboarding:

1. Create a soul sigil.
2. Choose an archetype.
3. Enter a small neglected sanctuary.
4. Pick one daily ritual.
5. Log or simulate one real-world action.
6. Gather the first material.
7. Craft or restore one object.
8. See the sanctuary change.
9. Receive one clear next step for tomorrow.

Example:

```text
You choose the Sanctuary Keeper archetype.

Your first ritual:
"Tidy one small surface."

You complete it.

The room grows still. Dust gathers into a pinch of Order Salt.

You gained:
- 1 Order Salt
- 1 Stone Resonance

You use Order Salt to repair the cracked threshold ward.

The Dormant Sanctuary is now: Slightly Stable.
```

This is stronger than giving the player 10 XP. The real-world action becomes part of the world.

## Main Screens

### 1. Sanctuary

The Sanctuary is the home screen and emotional anchor.

It shows:

- Character name and archetype.
- Current day or season.
- Current Focus or daily action capacity.
- Sanctuary condition.
- Active rituals.
- Gathered resources.
- Crafting recommendations.
- Recent world events.
- Mentor or spirit messages.

The Sanctuary should answer:

- What is my state today?
- What can I do gently and meaningfully?
- What did my recent life activity create?
- What can I craft or restore next?

### 2. Ritual Log

The Ritual Log replaces a generic habit list.

It contains real-world actions the player chooses to recognize.

Ritual types:

- Movement.
- Rest.
- Nourishment.
- Study.
- Reflection.
- Care.
- Household.
- Planning.
- Creativity.
- Connection.

Each ritual has:

- Name.
- Domain.
- Frequency.
- Gentle target.
- Symbolic interpretation.
- Possible gathered resources.
- Associated attunements.

Example:

```text
Ritual: Evening Reset
Domain: Household
Frequency: Daily
Action: Tidy one area for 5 minutes
Gathering: Order Salt, Entropy Ash, Steward's Wax
Attunement: Stone, Thread
```

### 3. Gathering Results

After logging a ritual, the player sees an interpretive result.

Example:

```text
You walked under a gray sky and followed the ley road past the old mile stones.

Gathered:
- 2 Ember Moss
- 1 Road Dust

Resonance:
- Fire +1
- Thread +1
```

Results should feel authored and magical, not mechanical. Avoid obvious one-to-one quantified-self phrasing.

### 4. Inventory

Inventory is where progress becomes tangible.

Initial categories:

- Materials.
- Essences.
- Crafted items.
- Relics.
- Ritual tools.
- Sanctuary objects.
- Story keys.

Later categories:

- Contracts.
- Commodity fragments.
- Guild tokens.
- Soulbound achievements.

### 5. Crafting Bench

The Crafting Bench is the main interaction screen for turning resources into meaning.

Initial crafting outputs:

- Potions.
- Charms.
- Sanctuary repairs.
- Study sigils.
- Hearth tools.
- Dream items.
- Small relics.

Example recipes:

```text
Sleep Draught
Requires:
- 2 Lucid Dew
- 1 Stillwater

Effect:
- Restores morale

```

```text
Threshold Ward
Requires:
- 2 Order Salt
- 1 Steward's Wax

Effect:
- Improves Sanctuary Stability
- Unlocks the Store Room
```

### 6. Rituals

Rituals are structured magical actions that require materials, timing, consistency, or attunement.

They are more significant than basic crafting.

Examples:

- Stabilize a leyline through seven days of walking.
- Bind a household sanctuary with hearthlight.
- Create a Study Sigil from three learning sessions.
- Brew a Sleep Draught from lucid dew.
- Restore a neglected room using order materials.

Rituals can be solo in the MVP. Later they can become guild or covenant activities.

### 7. Mentor Hall

Mentors guide domains of life and provide story.

In the new direction, mentors should not only issue tasks. They should interpret life activity, teach crafting recipes, introduce attunements, and help the player choose rituals.

Mentor functions:

- Introduce domains.
- Offer gentle ritual suggestions.
- Teach recipes.
- Unlock areas.
- Interpret failure and recovery.
- Provide story arcs.
- Eventually witness contracts.

The map should start small.

Initial regions:

- Sanctuary.

- Dream Garden.

- Stillwater Shrine.
- Store Room.

Each region corresponds to real-world domains and resource types.

The map does not need real-time movement. It can be a list of text locations with available actions.

### 9. Codex

The Codex explains lore and systems.

Sections:

- Materials.
- Attunements.
- Archetypes.
- Recipes.
- Rituals.
- Mentors.
- Sanctuary history.

The crypto mapping should remain optional and late.

## Crafting

Crafting is the first product's main payoff.

Players gather materials from life rituals and use them to create objects that change the game world.

Crafting categories:

- Potions.
- Charms.
- Relics.
- Sanctuary objects.
- Study tools.
- Hearth tools.
- Ward stones.
- Ritual components.

Crafting should be useful in three ways:

1. **Mechanical:** restore morale, unlock rooms, improve gathering, increase Focus capacity.
2. **Narrative:** reveal story, mentor memories, sanctuary history.
3. **Identity:** reinforce archetype, attunement, and player style.

## Focus, Rhythm, and Limits

Pitchfork should avoid infinite task farming.

### Focus

Focus is the daily game action resource.

Players can earn Focus through rituals, but it should be capped.

Focus is spent on:

- Exploring regions.
- Crafting complex items.
- Performing rituals.
- Consulting mentors.
- Investigating story events.

### Participation Caps

Hard caps are important.

Examples:

- Maximum Focus earned per day.
- Maximum raw materials from repeated ritual type.
- Weekly bonuses instead of infinite daily farming.
- Diminishing returns for repeated identical actions.

This prevents:

- Optimization pressure.
- Behavioral extraction.
- Feeling punished for limited time or energy.
- Turning life into grind.

### Rest as Valid Play

Rest must be a valid action, not a failure state.

The product should include:

- Rest rituals.
- Recovery days.
- Pause modes.
- Gentle streak handling.
- Forgiveness mechanics.
- Low-energy recommendations.

## Contracts and Covenants

Contracts are still central to Pitchfork, but they should not be the first emotional hook.

They should unlock after the player understands:

- Rituals.
- Gathering.
- Inventory.
- Crafting.
- Sanctuary progression.

Then contracts can formalize commitments.

In the new language, contracts become **Binding Covenants** or **Sealed Rituals**.

### Basic Covenant

```text
Covenant: Tend the Ley Road
Promise: Walk three times before Sunday.
Seal: 1 Ember Moss
Reward: 1 Vitality Charm recipe, Fire Resonance +2
Failure: Seal remains dormant until next week.
Witness: Kiera Fitflame
```

### Why Covenants Matter

Covenants introduce:

- Commitment.
- Escrow.
- Settlement.
- Witnessing.
- Reputation.
- Later smart contract logic.

They should feel like a natural extension of ritual practice, not a productivity contract form.

The first economy should be internal, symbolic, and capped.

### Materials

Raw materials come from rituals:

- Ember Moss.
- Lucid Dew.
- Order Salt.
- Rune Fragments.
- Hearthlight.
- Memory Ink.
- Road Dust.

### Essences

Essences are more refined resources:

- Fire Essence.
- Dream Essence.
- Hearth Essence.
- Study Essence.
- Care Essence.
- Gold Essence.

Essences can be crafted from materials or earned through meaningful ritual patterns.

### Crafted Items

Crafted items are the first higher-order economic objects:

- Sleep Draught.
- Threshold Ward.
- Study Sigil.
- Vitality Charm.
- Caretaker's Lantern.
- Ledger Stone.
- Hearth Bowl.

### Soulbound Achievements

Some things should never be tradable, even in future multiplayer:

- Personal milestones.
- Recovery arcs.
- Archetype evolution.
- Mentor trust.
- Covenant reputation.
- Major sanctuary restorations.

### Future Trade

Trading should come later and focus on crafted symbolic value, not raw extraction of behavior.

The healthier path is:

```text
life participation -> capped materials -> crafted items -> optional trade
```

not:

```text
more tracked behavior -> more money
```

## Verification Philosophy

The system should be symbolic, not forensic.

The MVP can use self-attestation:

- "I did this."
- "I rested."
- "I cleaned."
- "I studied."
- "I cared for someone."

This is acceptable because the app is first a personal ritual system, not a wage platform.

Later integrations may support optional verification:

- Step count.
- Sleep import.
- Calendar sessions.
- Timer sessions.
- Manual journal entries.

But verification should never become invasive by default.

Important principle:

> Pitchfork should not become surveillance infrastructure.

## Safety and Ethics

The product must avoid:

- Surveillance capitalism.
- Gamified domestic extraction.
- Productivity coercion.
- Behavioral wage labor.
- Health-data speculation.
- Manipulative streak addiction.
- Exploitative token farming.
- Gender-role reinforcement.

Design safeguards:

- Hard reward caps.
- Privacy by default.
- Manual entry as a first-class path.
- Sensitive domains opt-in only.
- No public activity feed in the MVP.
- Rest and recovery mechanics.
- Soulbound non-tradable identity markers.
- Clear separation between symbolic rewards and any future financial layer.
- Multiple valid play styles.

Sensitive domains require special care:

- Menstrual cycle tracking.
- Health data.
- Mental health.
- Location.
- Household and caregiving data.
- Financial data.

These should not be MVP requirements.

## Future MMO Vision

The eventual MMO is not combat-first. It is a persistent magical civilization where players' real-world activities animate shared spaces.

The inspiration is closer to a Zwift-like shared presence model than a traditional raid-and-loot MMO:

> Real-world effort becomes visible shared presence.

Players could see:

- Walkers moving along ley roads.
- Scholars researching in towers.
- Caretakers maintaining sanctuaries.
- Alchemists brewing at hearths.
- Guilds performing rituals.
- Caravans moving between cities.
- Dreamers stabilizing moon gates.
- Gardeners cultivating resource zones.

### Passive Presence

The player appears in the world based on ordinary activity.

Examples:

- Walking moves an avatar along roads.
- Sleep sends the avatar into dream realms.
- Study places the avatar in a library.
- Cooking lights a hearth.
- Cleaning stabilizes a sanctuary.
- Meditation places the avatar at a void shrine.

### Ritual Presence

Players gather for scheduled rituals.

Examples:

- Full moon ceremonies.
- Guild crafting events.
- Group walks.
- Synchronized meditation.
- Seasonal harvests.
- Sanctuary restoration.
- Community feasts.

### Economic Presence

Players contribute materials, crafts, services, and reputation to a shared economy.

Examples:

- Selling crafted potions.
- Contributing to guild vaults.
- Funding escape grants.
- Supporting household stewards.
- Trading rare resources.
- Commissioning ritual objects.

This MMO vision should not drive the MVP architecture prematurely, but the first product should avoid choices that make it impossible later.

## Product Risks

### The Game Feels Like a Habit Tracker

Mitigation:

- Lead with Sanctuary, gathering, and crafting.
- Use symbolic language.
- Make resources and rooms matter.
- Avoid generic task UI as the center.

### The Game Feels Too Abstract

Mitigation:

- Keep the first resources concrete.
- Make the first craft immediately visible.
- Use clear room restoration goals.
- Start with only a few domains.

### Players Feel Judged

Mitigation:

- Support rest.
- Avoid harsh streak loss.
- Use recovery rituals.
- Frame missed days as weather, not moral failure.

### Players Farm Meaningless Actions

Mitigation:

- Use daily caps.
- Use diminishing returns.
- Reward varied rhythms.
- Keep high-value progress tied to recipes and story, not repeated clicking.

### The Crypto Layer Arrives Too Early

Mitigation:

- Keep wallets, markets, and contracts out of onboarding.
- Introduce Commerce World through mystery.
- Use fantasy-native language.
- Let players love the world before explaining the analogy.

## What Makes This Direction Stronger

The previous app direction centered on tasks becoming contracts. That is still useful for the Pitchfork accounting layer, but it risks making the RPG client feel like a productivity app.

The new direction centers on life activity becoming magical material.

That gives Pitchfork a more distinctive identity:

- Walking gathers.
- Sleep restores.
- Cleaning stabilizes.
- Cooking nourishes.
- Studying reveals.
- Caregiving strengthens.
- Rest matters.
- Crafting turns life into world change.

Contracts can still become a major system, but they should emerge after the player already believes in the world.
