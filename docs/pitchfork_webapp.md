# Pitchfork Web App Product Concept

## Product Summary

Pitchfork should begin as a single-player web RPG where real-life habits and tasks become magical contracts. The player completes commitments, earns colored tokens, spends those tokens on character progression and exploration, and gradually unlocks deeper magical-economic systems.

The simplest useful version is a cross between:

- **Kingdom of Loathing:** text-forward RPG structure, daily turns, quirky locations, inventory, quests, simple actions, and low-friction play.
- **Habitica:** real-life habits, dailies, and todos drive character progress.
- **Pitchfork:** tokens, contracts, essence, blankening, escrow, and conversion teach cryptocurrency concepts through fantasy mechanics.

The product should not start as a large open-world RPG or a real blockchain app. It should start as a focused daily web game about making and keeping magical commitments.

## Core Promise

Pitchfork helps players turn ordinary life commitments into a fantasy progression loop.

The player is not just checking off tasks. They are:

- Training under mentors.
- Earning magical tokens.
- Drafting contracts.
- Managing scarce daily energy.
- Transforming specialized rewards into universal essence.
- Unlocking story chapters and new systems.
- Learning how value, trust, incentives, and automation work.

The central feeling should be:

> "I kept a promise to myself, and the game world responded."

## Product Positioning

Pitchfork is not:

- A generic todo list.
- A productivity dashboard with game cosmetics.
- A crypto wallet tutorial.
- A speculative finance game.
- A full blockchain integration at launch.

Pitchfork is:

- A personal progression RPG.
- A habit and contract game.
- A magical resource economy.
- A structured self-improvement system.
- A covert educational tool for crypto and economic concepts.

The player-facing language should remain magical. Crypto concepts should exist in the mechanics before they appear in the vocabulary.

## Player Fantasy

The player is a novice at the Institute of Mystical Mastery. Every real-world commitment they complete strengthens their magical discipline. Mentors reward them with colored tokens, which can be spent, saved, transformed, pledged into contracts, or converted into essence.

At first, the institute feels like a gentle magical school. Over time, the player discovers hidden systems: blank contracts, a shared ledger, commodity essence, automated pacts, and the Ethereal Nexus. The player's personal growth becomes tied to the fate of a magical economy.

## Design Principles

### Fun First

The app must feel rewarding even if the player never thinks about crypto. The game should stand on writing, progression, collection, satisfying actions, mentor relationships, and useful habit loops.

### One Daily Dashboard

The first version should have one strong daily home screen rather than many complex areas. The dashboard can link to locations, but the player should always know what to do today.

### Progress Is Inventory

Do not reduce everything to XP. The player earns objects: tokens, essence, blank contracts, commodity fragments, seals, mentor marks, and story keys. These objects create choices.

### Commitments Have Shape

A normal habit tracker asks, "Did you do it?" Pitchfork asks, "What promise did you make, what did you stake, who witnessed it, what happens if you keep it, and what happens if you fail?"

### Complexity Unlocks Slowly

The game begins with habits and colored tokens. Contracts, blankening, essence, escrow, commodities, automation, and the Nexus unlock later.

## Core Daily Loop

Each day, the player:

1. Opens the Institute dashboard.
2. Reviews today's dailies, habits, and active contracts.
3. Completes real-life tasks.
4. Claims token rewards and Focus.
5. Spends Focus on in-game actions.
6. Uses the Workbench to transform resources.
7. Advances mentor affinity, story scenes, and character stats.
8. Plans or drafts future contracts.

This creates two connected loops:

- **Real-life loop:** do tasks -> check them off -> maintain streaks -> improve life.
- **Game loop:** gain resources -> make choices -> unlock systems -> progress story.

## Main Screens

### 1. Today at the Institute

The main dashboard.

Shows:

- Character name, level, health/morale, Focus, and current day.
- Today's dailies.
- Active contracts due soon.
- Quick habit buttons.
- Mentor messages.
- Token inventory summary.
- Story/event feed.
- Recommended next action.

This screen should answer three questions immediately:

- What should I do today?
- What will I earn?
- What can I spend or unlock?

### 2. Contract Board

The place where commitments become formal game objects.

Sections:

- Available mentor contracts.
- Player-created contracts.
- Active contracts.
- Completed contracts.
- Failed or expired contracts.

Each contract displays:

- Title.
- Mentor or issuer.
- Task requirements.
- Deadline.
- Reward.
- Optional escrow/stake.
- Failure consequence.
- Status.
- Completion method.

At first, completion can be self-attested. Later versions can add proof types.

### 3. Mentor Hall

A relationship and quest hub.

Each mentor has:

- Portrait.
- Domain.
- Current affinity level.
- Active quest line.
- Available contracts.
- Token color affinities.
- Unlockable spells or features.

Mentors are how the game turns self-improvement categories into characters and story.

### 4. Inventory

The player's tangible progress.

Sections:

- Colored tokens by color and rarity.
- Essence Tokens.
- Blank contracts.
- Active contract seals.
- Commodity fragments.
- Story keys.
- Mentor marks.

Inventory should be clean and visual. The player should feel like they own useful magical objects, not abstract points.

### 5. Workbench

The signature transformation screen.

Initial actions:

- Blanken colored tokens into Essence Tokens.
- Combine tokens into simple spell boosts.
- Fill a blank contract with terms.

Later actions:

- Stake essence into contracts.
- Convert essence into commodity positions.
- Convert commodities into colored tokens.
- Create Automaton Pacts.
- Inspect contract history.

The Workbench is where the educational layer becomes tactile.

### 6. Locations

Small text-based RPG areas.

Initial locations:

- Dormitory.
- Mentor Hall.
- Contract Board.
- Workbench.
- Hidden Library.

Later locations:

- Market Atrium.
- Commodity Vault.
- Ethereal Nexus.
- Convergence Zones.

Each location offers a few actions, short flavor text, and possible rewards. This gives the Kingdom of Loathing flavor without requiring a huge map.

### 7. Codex

An optional knowledge base.

Contains:

- Lore entries.
- Spell descriptions.
- Token explanations.
- Contract concepts.
- Mentor backstories.
- Eventually, crypto concept mappings.

The Codex should be optional. It supports curious players without interrupting the main game.

## Task Types

Pitchfork should support three primary task types.

### Habits

Repeatable actions the player can do multiple times.

Examples:

- Drink water.
- Stretch.
- Read.
- Review finances.
- Message a friend.
- Meditate.

Habits give small rewards. They are useful for frequent positive reinforcement.

Recommended mechanics:

- Can be completed multiple times per day.
- Rewards are small.
- Streaks grant occasional bonuses.
- Overuse should be limited by diminishing returns or daily caps.

### Dailies

Expected once-per-day actions.

Examples:

- Exercise.
- Plan tomorrow.
- Track spending.
- Sleep routine.
- Study session.

Dailies provide structure and rhythm. Missing them should matter, but punishment should be careful. The game should avoid making players feel worse during already-bad days.

Recommended mechanics:

- Due once per day.
- Can have skip/rest settings.
- Completion gives tokens and Focus.
- Misses reduce morale, contract reliability, or streaks rather than harshly damaging the player.

### Contracts

Formal commitments with terms.

Examples:

- Complete three workouts before Sunday.
- Finish tax prep by Friday.
- Study Spanish five days this week.
- Cook at home four times.
- Spend two Focus exploring the Hidden Library after completing two learning tasks.

Contracts are the heart of Pitchfork's identity.

A contract has:

- Objective.
- Issuer.
- Deadline.
- Reward.
- Optional escrow.
- Optional failure condition.
- Mentor domain.
- Completion proof.
- Status history.

Contracts can be one-time, recurring, or multi-step.

## Contract Mechanics

### Basic Contract

The simplest contract:

```text
Contract: Walk for 20 minutes.
Issuer: Kiera Fitflame.
Due: Today.
Reward: 1 Red Token, 1 Focus.
Verification: Self-attested.
```

### Staked Contract

A stronger commitment:

```text
Contract: Train three times before Sunday.
Issuer: Kiera Fitflame.
Escrow: 2 Essence.
Reward: 3 Red Tokens, 1 Green Token.
Failure: Lose 1 escrowed Essence.
Verification: Self-attested checklist.
```

### Multi-Step Contract

A larger task:

```text
Contract: Prepare monthly budget.
Issuer: Arlo Goldweaver.
Steps:
1. Review last month's spending.
2. Categorize expenses.
3. Set three budget targets.
4. Schedule next review.
Reward: 2 Yellow Tokens, 1 Essence, Arlo Affinity +3.
```

### Contract Statuses

Suggested statuses:

- Draft.
- Active.
- Ready to settle.
- Completed.
- Failed.
- Expired.
- Forgiven.
- Disputed, for later versions.

### Settlement

Settlement is the moment a contract resolves. Rewards are paid, escrow is returned or forfeited, and the outcome is recorded in the player's ledger.

In the early version, settlement can be simple. Later, it can become one of the main teaching points for smart contracts.

## Token Economy

### Token Colors

- **Red:** action, courage, exertion.
- **Orange:** transformation, adaptation, momentum.
- **Yellow:** clarity, healing, protection.
- **Green:** growth, nourishment, sustainability.
- **Blue:** learning, flow, patience.
- **Purple:** perception, creativity, social influence.

### Token Rarity

Start with common tokens only. Add rarity later.

Possible tiers:

- Common.
- Rare.
- Legendary.

Rarity can be earned through longer contracts, streaks, story milestones, or difficult mentor quests.

### Essence Tokens

Essence Tokens are gray universal tokens created by blankening colored tokens.

Initial rule:

```text
1 colored token -> 1 Essence Token
```

Later balancing can make rarity, mentor affinity, or Workbench upgrades affect conversion.

### Why Essence Matters

Colored tokens are specialized. Essence is flexible.

A red token might help with fitness spells or action contracts. Essence can be used anywhere: escrow, crafting, unlocking features, drafting contracts, or market conversion.

This teaches the tradeoff between specialized utility and fungible liquidity.

## Focus, Energy, and Daily Limits

Pitchfork needs a daily action economy to avoid becoming an infinite checklist.

### Focus

Focus is the main daily action resource.

Players earn Focus by completing real-life tasks. They spend Focus on in-game actions:

- Explore a location.
- Train with a mentor.
- Study a Codex entry.
- Draft a complex contract.
- Work at the Workbench.
- Investigate the Nexus.

Example:

```text
Complete "morning walk" -> gain 1 Red Token and 2 Focus.
Spend 2 Focus exploring the Hidden Library.
Find 1 Blank Contract.
```

### Morale

Morale can represent the player's overall stability. It should be gentle.

Morale decreases from missed dailies or failed contracts. It increases from rest, recovery tasks, mentor support, and completing commitments.

Avoid harsh death spirals. If morale drops, the game should offer recovery contracts rather than punishments.

### Daily Reset

At reset:

- New dailies become due.
- Expired contracts settle.
- Focus resets or partially carries over.
- Mentor messages update.
- Streaks update.
- New location events appear.

## Progression

### Character Level

Level increases through contract completion, mentor quests, and story progress.

Level unlocks:

- More active contracts.
- More inventory capacity.
- New Workbench recipes.
- New locations.
- New mentor arcs.

### Mentor Affinity

Each mentor has an affinity level.

Affinity unlocks:

- Better contracts.
- New story scenes.
- Token bonuses.
- Spells.
- Mentor-specific Workbench recipes.

### Skill Tracks

Each token color can have a skill track.

Using and earning that color increases mastery. Higher mastery can improve rewards or unlock spells.

### Story Chapters

Story progression should be gated by system understanding, not only raw level.

Example gates:

- Earn one token from three mentors.
- Complete your first contract.
- Blanken your first token.
- Settle a staked contract.
- Unlock the Nexus ledger.

## Later Systems

### Commodities

After the core habit-contract loop works, introduce commodities.

Example:

- Spend Essence to buy Wheat Essence.
- Wheat can later convert into Green Tokens.
- Market conditions affect conversion rates.

Keep initial markets fictional and deterministic. Real market data is unnecessary for the MVP.

### Automaton Pacts

Automaton Pacts are recurring or conditional contracts.

Examples:

- If I complete three study sessions this week, automatically convert 1 Essence into a Blue Token.
- If I miss two sleep dailies, offer a recovery contract.

These model smart contract automation.

### Simulated NPC Economy

The player can interact with NPC contract issuers, traders, and factions. This creates the feeling of a living economy before multiplayer exists.

### Seasonal Arcs

Limited-time story events can encourage specific domains:

- Harvest season for nutrition and planning.
- Festival of Bonds for social connection.
- Ledgerfall for contracts and cleanup.
- Ember Trials for fitness and action.

## Onboarding

The first session should take less than five minutes.

Suggested flow:

1. Choose character name.
2. Pick one goal area.
3. Meet one mentor.
4. Create one daily.
5. Complete or simulate first task.
6. Receive first colored token.
7. See inventory.
8. Spend one Focus on a tiny story action.

Do not ask the player to configure their entire life up front. Start with one commitment.

## Example First Session

The player chooses "Mira" as their character name.

The game asks:

```text
What brought you to the Institute?

- I want more energy.
- I want better focus.
- I want to feel organized.
- I want to understand money.
- I want steadier habits.
```

The player chooses "I want more energy."

Kiera Fitflame appears and offers:

```text
First Ember Contract
Walk, stretch, or move for 10 minutes today.
Reward: 1 Red Token, 2 Focus.
```

The player accepts. After completing it, the event feed says:

```text
Kiera presses a warm ember-token into your palm.
"You kept your promise to your body. That matters."

You gained 1 Red Token.
You gained 2 Focus.
```

The player spends 1 Focus to visit the Dormitory and receives a short story beat. The session ends with one clear next step for tomorrow.

The player completes the walk and reading task, gains rewards, blankens one Blue Token into Essence, and spends 2 Focus exploring the Hidden Library. They find a Blank Contract and unlock the next Nexus story hint.

That is already a complete product loop.

## Rewards and Balance

### Reward Sizing

Early rewards should be small and frequent.

Suggested baseline:

- Small habit: 1 token fragment or small Focus reward.
- Daily: 1 token and 1-2 Focus.
- Simple contract: 1-3 tokens.
- Multi-step contract: 3-6 tokens, Essence, affinity.
- Story milestone: rare token, spell, or feature unlock.

### Avoiding Exploits

Players can always cheat in self-attested habit apps. The goal is not perfect enforcement. The goal is meaningful self-contracting.

Mitigations:

- Limit rewards per task per day.
- Use escalating rewards for consistency, not spam.
- Make high-value contracts require escrow or multi-step progress.
- Make rewards configurable but bounded.
- Let players mark tasks as easy, normal, or hard.
- Encourage honest reflection through mentor dialogue.

### Failure Design

Failure should create recovery, not abandonment.

When a player misses tasks:

- Offer a smaller recovery contract.
- Let them rest without breaking everything.
- Reduce streaks or contract reliability gently.
- Avoid severe punishment.
- Preserve long-term progress.

The product should support people through imperfect weeks.

## Product Risks

### Too Much Complexity Too Early

If contracts, essence, mentors, inventory, and story all arrive immediately, players may bounce. The first session should use one mentor, one task, one token.

### Feeling Like Homework

If the app is too productivity-focused, it loses the fantasy. The writing, rewards, inventory, and story feed need charm.

### Feeling Like Fake Productivity

If the game rewards meaningless clicks, users will lose trust. Rewards should be tied to commitments the player actually values.

### Crypto Association

Some users distrust crypto. Keep the early product fantasy-first and avoid wallet language. The concepts can be present without the baggage.

### Punishment Spiral

Habit apps can make people feel worse when they fall behind. Pitchfork should include rest, forgiveness, and recovery mechanics from the beginning.

## What Makes Pitchfork Different

Habitica turns tasks into RPG rewards. Pitchfork turns tasks into **contracts and resources**.

That distinction matters:

- A task can be casual or binding.
- A reward can be held, spent, blankened, or staked.
- A commitment can have escrow.
- A failed agreement can settle.
- A history can become a ledger.
- A habit loop can become a personal economy.

Pitchfork's unique identity is not just "habits plus fantasy." It is **personal commitments as magical economic objects**.
