# Pitchfork RPG Design Goals

## Purpose

The Pitchfork RPG exists to explore how symbolic worlds can help people understand, improve, and participate in real-world systems of cooperation.

Rather than treating economics, governance, stewardship, and community organization as abstract subjects, the RPG presents them as discoverable features of a magical world. Players learn through exploration, experimentation, and collaboration instead of formal instruction.

The project is therefore both a game and a research platform.

Every design decision should support one or more of the goals described below.

---

# Primary Design Goal

The primary goal of the Pitchfork RPG is to cultivate players who understand the symbolic language of the Pitchfork ecosystem well enough to extend it.

The ideal player does not merely complete quests or optimize character builds.

Instead they eventually ask questions such as:

* Could this magical institution work better?
* What if contracts behaved differently?
* Could a new magical discipline solve this problem?
* What incentives would improve this guild?
* How should a commonwealth organize itself?

The RPG should produce contributors rather than consumers.

---

# A Living Research Platform

The game is intended to become an ongoing laboratory for experimentation.

Players should be encouraged to invent:

* magical schools,
* crafting systems,
* rituals,
* governance structures,
* economic institutions,
* symbolic resources,
* cooperative organizations,
* educational experiences,
* and entirely new forms of gameplay.

Successful ideas may eventually influence future development of the Pitchfork platform and the Pancakes Commonwealth.

The symbolic world is therefore not simply entertainment.

It is a safe place to explore ideas before applying them in real communities.

---

# Teach Through Discovery

The game should avoid presenting its ideas as lectures.

Instead, players discover increasingly sophisticated concepts as they progress through the world.

Each new magical discovery should correspond to a deeper conceptual abstraction.

Examples include:

* color magic,
* blankening,
* alchemy,
* contracts,
* institutions,
* governance,
* stewardship,
* civilization.

The player should experience genuine curiosity as they uncover these ideas.

The game should reward exploration and experimentation more than memorization.

---

# Make Complex Ideas Intuitive

Many important social concepts are difficult to explain directly.

The RPG uses symbolic language to make them intuitive.

Examples include:

| Real Concept            | Symbolic Representation |
| ----------------------- | ----------------------- |
| Value                   | Magical essence         |
| Agreements              | Covenants               |
| Contracts               | Magical bindings        |
| Reputation              | Renown                  |
| Institutions            | Guilds and councils     |
| Shared infrastructure   | Sanctuaries             |
| Governance              | Mage councils           |
| Resource transformation | Alchemy                 |
| Economic exchange       | Magical commerce        |

The goal is not to disguise these ideas.

The goal is to make them easier to understand through play.

---

# Stewardship Before Power

Many games reward conquest.

The Pitchfork RPG should primarily reward stewardship.

Players should feel satisfaction from:

* building,
* maintaining,
* restoring,
* teaching,
* crafting,
* organizing,
* caring,
* cooperating,
* and improving shared institutions.

Combat may exist, but it should not define the identity of the world.

The greatest accomplishments should involve helping civilizations flourish rather than defeating increasingly powerful enemies.

---

# Recognize Invisible Value

Modern economies often overlook forms of work that are essential to healthy communities.

The RPG should recognize activities such as:

* caregiving,
* household management,
* education,
* maintenance,
* emotional support,
* community organization,
* long-term stewardship,
* recovery,
* reflection,
* and creativity.

Within the symbolic world these activities become meaningful forms of magical participation rather than background labor.

---

# Encourage Cooperation

The strongest achievements should emerge through collaboration.

Players should naturally form:

* guilds,
* covenants,
* research groups,
* households,
* communities,
* teaching circles,
* and mutual aid networks.

The design should encourage trust, reciprocity, and long-term relationships rather than purely competitive optimization.

---

# Encourage Multiple Ways To Flourish

There should never be a single "correct" path through the game.

Different players should be able to specialize in different forms of contribution.

Examples include:

* scholars,
* explorers,
* artisans,
* healers,
* organizers,
* household stewards,
* traders,
* ritualists,
* researchers,
* gardeners,
* teachers.

The world should celebrate diversity of contribution rather than forcing every player into the same progression system.

---

# Build A Safe Economic Sandbox

The symbolic economy should allow experimentation without exposing participants to unnecessary financial risk.

Players should be able to explore:

* currencies,
* contracts,
* exchange,
* reputation,
* institutions,
* incentives,
* governance,
* resource allocation,
* and cooperation.

The purpose is to understand how systems behave before introducing real-world economic consequences.

The symbolic economy should therefore be treated as a research environment rather than a speculative marketplace.

---

# Preserve Human Agency

The game should support people rather than optimizing them.

Players should always retain meaningful control over their participation.

The system should avoid encouraging:

* surveillance,
* coercion,
* addictive optimization,
* endless grinding,
* productivity obsession,
* or exploitative reward structures.

Instead it should encourage:

* curiosity,
* reflection,
* moderation,
* creativity,
* collaboration,
* and voluntary participation.

---

# Encourage Extensibility

The RPG should be designed as an extensible platform rather than a fixed game.

Future contributors should be able to add:

* magical schools,
* resources,
* recipes,
* creatures,
* factions,
* rituals,
* quests,
* educational modules,
* research projects,
* and entirely new clients.

The symbolic world should continuously evolve without requiring the underlying architecture to change fundamentally.

---

# Validate Pitchfork

The RPG serves as one of the primary validation environments for the Pitchfork platform.

New ideas can first be implemented symbolically.

Questions that can be explored include:

* Does this accounting model feel intuitive?
* Does this contract primitive create interesting gameplay?
* Is this governance mechanism understandable?
* Does this resource system encourage healthy cooperation?
* Can players explain these ideas to one another?

The RPG therefore acts as an experimental client for the broader Pitchfork ecosystem.

---

# Long-Term Vision

The long-term objective is not to build the largest fantasy game.

It is to cultivate a community capable of thinking about stewardship, economics, institutions, and civilization through a shared symbolic language.

As players deepen their understanding, they become capable of extending both the fictional world and the real-world systems that inspired it.

The greatest success of the Pitchfork RPG will not be measured by the number of monsters defeated or items collected.

It will be measured by whether players begin proposing better ways for communities to cooperate, govern themselves, care for one another, and build a flourishing commonwealth together.
