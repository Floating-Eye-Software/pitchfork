# World and Setting

## Introduction

The world of the Pitchfork RPG is an ancient magical civilization built upon centuries of accumulated knowledge.

To new students, it appears to be a traditional fantasy world filled with mentors, magical schools, enchanted forests, forgotten ruins, mysterious relics, and powerful rituals.

As their understanding grows, players gradually discover that the visible world is only one interpretation of a much deeper symbolic reality.

The setting is therefore intentionally layered.

Every location has both narrative significance and conceptual meaning.

---

# The Nature of the World

The world is old.

Long before the arrival of the player, countless generations of mages, scholars, artisans, explorers, and stewards have expanded magical knowledge.

Civilizations have risen.

Institutions have flourished.

Mistakes have been made.

Knowledge has been forgotten.

The player enters a world that is already alive.

They are not the first hero.

They are the next generation.

---

# A Living Civilization

Unlike many fantasy settings, the world is not defined by an endless war between good and evil.

Its greatest achievements are:

* libraries,
* academies,
* guilds,
* workshops,
* observatories,
* marketplaces,
* sanctuaries,
* gardens,
* households,
* and communities.

Civilization itself is treated as humanity's greatest magical achievement.

---

# The Academy

Every player begins at the Academy.

The Academy is one of the oldest institutions in the world.

Its purpose is not merely to teach spellcasting.

It preserves accumulated magical knowledge while preparing future generations to extend it.

Students learn:

* magical theory,
* practical spellcraft,
* alchemy,
* rituals,
* history,
* ethics,
* stewardship,
* and research methods.

The Academy openly acknowledges that its understanding remains incomplete.

Discovery is considered part of every student's education.

---

# The Mentors

Each mentor represents an established tradition of magical knowledge.

Mentors are:

* teachers,
* researchers,
* historians,
* practitioners,
* and custodians.

They embody centuries of accumulated understanding while encouraging students to question assumptions responsibly.

No mentor possesses complete knowledge.

Each sees the world through a particular symbolic tradition.

Together they provide a diverse foundation for future discovery.

---

# The Magical Landscape

The world is filled with places shaped by magical history.

Examples include:

* ancient forests,
* mountain observatories,
* forgotten libraries,
* abandoned workshops,
* ritual circles,
* sanctuaries,
* crossroads,
* trade halls,
* rivers,
* caverns,
* gardens,
* and ruined cities.

Every location tells part of the world's history.

Many continue changing as players participate in the world.

---

# Sanctuaries

Sanctuaries are places of long-term stewardship.

Some are private.

Some belong to guilds.

Others serve entire communities.

A sanctuary represents more than a player home.

It is an expression of accumulated care.

Players gradually improve sanctuaries through:

* crafting,
* maintenance,
* research,
* decoration,
* cooperation,
* and ritual practice.

Over time, sanctuaries become living records of their caretakers.

---

# Guilds

Guilds preserve specialized knowledge.

Rather than existing solely for combat or competition, guilds focus upon:

* teaching,
* craftsmanship,
* exploration,
* research,
* trade,
* stewardship,
* and mutual support.

Guilds become one of the primary ways civilization remembers what previous generations have learned.

---

# The Commerce World

As novices progress, they encounter evidence of another symbolic tradition.

This civilization appears strangely different.

Its greatest accomplishments come not from elemental spellcasting but from:

* agreements,
* institutions,
* coordination,
* ownership,
* governance,
* and exchange.

Initially this world feels almost alien.

Later the player realizes it has been connected to the magical world all along.

The distinction exists primarily within the player's understanding.

---

# The Convergence

One of the defining events of the setting is the gradual convergence of magical and institutional knowledge.

As researchers uncover deeper truths:

magic learns from commerce,

and commerce learns from magic.

The resulting civilization becomes richer than either tradition alone.

This convergence continues throughout the lifetime of the world.

It is never truly complete.

---

# Time

The world changes continuously.

Seasons pass.

Guilds evolve.

Communities grow.

Research produces discoveries.

Players contribute new knowledge.

The setting is intended to evolve alongside its community rather than remaining permanently fixed.

History continues to be written.

---

# The Commonwealth

The highest expression of civilization is the Commonwealth.

The Commonwealth is not an empire.

It is a network of communities capable of cooperating while preserving local identity.

Its strength comes from:

* stewardship,
* trust,
* institutions,
* mutual aid,
* shared knowledge,
* craftsmanship,
* and long-term thinking.

Players gradually come to understand that the Commonwealth represents a living magical achievement rather than a political conquest.

---

# Walden Three

Walden Three is one example of a mature Commonwealth community.

It represents a society that has successfully integrated:

* magic,
* stewardship,
* contracts,
* institutions,
* education,
* cooperation,
* and care.

It is not presented as a perfect society.

Nor is it the inevitable future.

Instead, it demonstrates what becomes possible when generations of people devote themselves to cultivating a flourishing civilization.

Players are encouraged to learn from Walden Three while imagining and building new communities of their own.

---

# The Player's Place In The World

The player does not arrive to save civilization.

Civilization already exists.

The player's responsibility is to understand it.

To improve it.

To preserve what is valuable.

To repair what is broken.

To discover what previous generations overlooked.

The player eventually becomes another contributor to the world's ongoing history.

---

# An Evolving Setting

The world of the Pitchfork RPG is intentionally unfinished.

New generations should be able to introduce:

* regions,
* cultures,
* magical traditions,
* guilds,
* creatures,
* rituals,
* institutions,
* research,
* and stories.

The world grows through participation.

Just as the Academy teaches that magical knowledge is never complete, the setting itself remains permanently open to discovery.

Every player enters an ancient world.

Every player leaves it slightly different than they found it.
