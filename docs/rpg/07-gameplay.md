# Gameplay

## Introduction

The Pitchfork RPG is a persistent browser-based role-playing game where players gather resources, practice magic, craft artifacts, build sanctuaries, cooperate with others, and gradually uncover the deeper structure of their world.

Unlike traditional RPGs, progression is driven primarily by exploration, craftsmanship, research, stewardship, and community participation rather than combat alone.

The player is not expected to save the world.

Instead, they become one of many people helping a living civilization continue to grow.

---

# Core Gameplay Loop

The game revolves around a simple repeating cycle.

1. Explore the world.
2. Gather magical resources.
3. Learn new magical knowledge.
4. Craft useful objects.
5. Improve your sanctuary.
6. Cooperate with other players.
7. Discover new magical concepts.
8. Contribute to the world.

This loop gradually expands throughout the game without fundamentally changing.

As the player matures, the scale of their participation grows from individual actions to entire communities.

---

# Exploration

Players travel through an interconnected world filled with:

* forests,
* mountains,
* rivers,
* villages,
* ruins,
* workshops,
* libraries,
* sanctuaries,
* marketplaces,
* guild halls,
* and ancient magical sites.

Exploration rewards curiosity.

Hidden places often contain:

* rare resources,
* forgotten knowledge,
* recipes,
* historical records,
* magical creatures,
* and new research opportunities.

The world is designed to encourage wandering rather than rushing toward objectives.

---

# Gathering

Resources are the foundation of magical civilization.

Players gather:

* plants,
* minerals,
* wood,
* water,
* fibers,
* metals,
* magical reagents,
* symbolic materials,
* and other natural resources.

Different regions produce different materials.

Seasonal changes, geography, and ecological conditions influence what may be gathered.

Gathering should feel like participating in a living ecosystem rather than harvesting abstract game resources.

---

# Crafting

Nearly every meaningful object in the world is crafted.

Players learn recipes for creating:

* tools,
* magical instruments,
* relics,
* books,
* furniture,
* workshops,
* contracts,
* sanctuaries,
* gardens,
* and countless other artifacts.

Crafting emphasizes creativity rather than repetitive production.

New discoveries continually expand what becomes possible.

---

# Research

Players are not limited to consuming existing knowledge.

Research allows them to:

* discover recipes,
* investigate magical phenomena,
* experiment with resources,
* test combinations,
* develop rituals,
* and contribute new understanding.

Research is a core gameplay activity rather than a background statistic.

The Academy provides a foundation.

Players continue the work.

---

# Magic

Magic accompanies nearly every activity.

Players learn:

* spells,
* rituals,
* alchemy,
* symbolic transformation,
* crafting techniques,
* contract magic,
* and institutional magic.

Magic should feel like a practical discipline rather than a collection of disconnected abilities.

New magical techniques often emerge through experimentation.

---

# Quests

Quests introduce players to the world while encouraging exploration.

Early quests are provided by mentors and local communities.

As the game progresses, quests increasingly emerge from:

* guilds,
* institutions,
* player communities,
* contracts,
* research,
* and world events.

Eventually many quests are created by the players themselves.

The world gradually shifts from scripted adventures toward living institutions.

---

# Sanctuaries

Every player develops one or more sanctuaries.

A sanctuary is:

* a home,
* a workshop,
* a research laboratory,
* a garden,
* a library,
* a gathering place,
* and a reflection of the player's journey.

Players gradually improve their sanctuaries through:

* construction,
* decoration,
* magical infrastructure,
* crafting,
* research,
* and stewardship.

Sanctuaries become long-term projects rather than temporary housing.

---

# Guilds

Guilds provide opportunities for long-term cooperation.

Players may join or establish guilds devoted to:

* magical research,
* exploration,
* craftsmanship,
* stewardship,
* teaching,
* ecological restoration,
* commerce,
* or entirely new purposes.

Guilds preserve knowledge and enable projects too large for individuals.

---

# Contracts

As players mature, contracts become an increasingly important gameplay system.

Contracts allow players and institutions to coordinate work.

Examples include:

* crafting commissions,
* exploration expeditions,
* research collaborations,
* infrastructure projects,
* stewardship responsibilities,
* educational programs,
* and community initiatives.

Contracts gradually become one of the primary ways civilization organizes itself.

---

# Economy

Resources circulate continuously through the world.

Players gather.

Craft.

Trade.

Donate.

Store.

Consume.

Transform.

The economy exists to support civilization rather than encourage endless accumulation.

Value is measured by usefulness and stewardship as much as scarcity.

---

# Progression

Progression emphasizes expanding understanding rather than increasing numerical power.

Players gradually unlock:

* magical disciplines,
* crafting knowledge,
* institutions,
* research opportunities,
* governance responsibilities,
* community leadership,
* and stewardship.

Knowledge becomes a more important form of progression than equipment alone.

---

# Cooperation

Many of the world's greatest achievements require cooperation.

Players work together to:

* build sanctuaries,
* restore ecosystems,
* establish guilds,
* conduct research,
* organize expeditions,
* preserve knowledge,
* and strengthen communities.

The game rewards collaboration without requiring constant multiplayer interaction.

Solo and cooperative play should both feel meaningful.

---

# Real-World Participation

The browser RPG is designed to coexist with the broader Pancakes ecosystem.

Depending on the client and player preferences, real-world participation may contribute symbolic resources to the game.

Examples include:

* walking,
* learning,
* cooking,
* gardening,
* journaling,
* caregiving,
* exercising,
* creating art,
* and community participation.

These activities are interpreted through the symbolic language of the magical world.

The goal is not to gamify everyday life.

The goal is to allow real experiences to enrich the player's ongoing story.

---

# A Persistent World

The world continues evolving whether or not any individual player is online.

Communities grow.

Guilds conduct research.

Institutions change.

Markets fluctuate.

New discoveries emerge.

Players participate in an ongoing civilization rather than repeatedly restarting isolated adventures.

---

# Many Ways to Play

There is no single correct style of play.

A player may become:

* an explorer,
* an alchemist,
* a scholar,
* a ritualist,
* a builder,
* a gardener,
* a healer,
* a steward,
* a merchant,
* a teacher,
* a researcher,
* or something entirely new.

The game should encourage players to discover their own role within the world.

---

# Long-Term Gameplay

As players approach the later stages of the game, their goals naturally shift.

Rather than pursuing stronger equipment or higher levels, they begin contributing to civilization itself.

They establish institutions.

Teach apprentices.

Create magical schools.

Develop new rituals.

Build communities.

Design contracts.

Improve governance.

Conduct research.

Expand the symbolic language of the world.

The ultimate progression of the Pitchfork RPG is therefore not unlimited personal power.

It is becoming a steward of a living civilization and helping future generations discover possibilities that did not previously exist.
