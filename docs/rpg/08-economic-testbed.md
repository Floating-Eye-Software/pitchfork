# The Economic Testbed

## Introduction

The Pitchfork RPG is more than a fantasy role-playing game.

It is a symbolic laboratory for exploring economics, institutions, governance, and stewardship.

Within the game, players experiment with magical systems.

Outside the game, those same symbolic systems represent ideas about how real communities cooperate, create value, organize institutions, and build civilizations.

The purpose of the testbed is not to prove a particular economic theory.

Its purpose is to provide a safe environment where competing ideas can be explored, observed, refined, and sometimes discarded.

---

# Why A Symbolic Economy?

Real economies affect real people.

Mistakes can cause hardship.

Experiments may have unintended consequences.

The symbolic world allows many of those ideas to be explored without placing participants at financial risk.

Players can ask questions such as:

* What happens if we change this incentive?
* How should magical resources circulate?
* Should this institution be decentralized?
* Does this covenant encourage cooperation?
* Is this governance model understandable?
* How should stewardship be recognized?

Within the symbolic world these become gameplay questions.

Outside the game they become research questions.

---

# A Sandbox For Institutions

Most economic simulations focus primarily on money.

The Pitchfork RPG deliberately models a much broader range of institutions.

Examples include:

* households,
* guilds,
* academies,
* marketplaces,
* councils,
* libraries,
* research communities,
* sanctuaries,
* mutual aid organizations,
* and commonwealths.

The interactions between these institutions are often more important than individual transactions.

# Studying Value

The symbolic economy allows players to investigate questions about value itself.

Examples include:

* What creates value?
* How is value recognized?
* Can value change form?
* Which contributions remain invisible?
* Which activities deserve stewardship?
* How should shared resources be maintained?

Magic provides a symbolic language for asking these questions without reducing every answer to money.

Researchers can explore questions such as:

Contracts provide one of the most important experimental systems.

# Stewardship

Traditional games often reward extraction.

The Pitchfork RPG intentionally studies stewardship.

Questions include:

* How should maintenance be recognized?
* How can restoration become rewarding?
* What encourages long-term thinking?
* How do communities preserve shared infrastructure?
* What institutions sustain healthy civilizations?

Players learn that maintaining a world is often more difficult than creating one.

# Participation Limits And Non-Transferable Meaning

Early designs proposed capped participation rewards: basic involvement could
provide access to symbolic resources, while additional optimization would not
produce unlimited income. This remains a useful experiment rather than a fixed
economic rule.

Possible safeguards include:

* limits by time period or activity class,
* diminishing or non-economic recognition,
* multiple valid paths including rest and recovery,
* no invasive proof requirement for low-stakes participation,
* and clear separation between symbolic rewards and real compensation.

Some outcomes should remain non-transferable. Personal milestones, trust,
teaching lineages, recovery journeys, and stewardship histories can retain
meaning precisely because they cannot be sold.

Communities may also test cooperative pools for mutual aid, education,
childcare, emergency support, shared infrastructure, and public projects.
Authority, eligibility, funding, and safeguards must be explicit; a compelling
game mechanic is not sufficient authorization for a real financial program.

---

# Research Rather Than Optimization

The objective is not to discover one perfect economy.

Different communities may require different institutions.

The testbed therefore encourages comparison rather than prescription.

Players are expected to ask:

* What worked?
* What failed?
* Why?
* Under what conditions?
* What assumptions proved incorrect?

Unexpected discoveries are often more valuable than confirming existing beliefs.

---

---

# Relationship To Pitchfork

The RPG does not replace the Pitchfork platform.

Instead it provides a symbolic interpretation of Pitchfork's underlying primitives.

Players interact with:

* magical resources,
* rituals,
* contracts,
* guilds,
* sanctuaries,
* and institutions.

Underneath these symbolic representations lie more general concepts such as:

* events,
* accounting,
* resources,
* recipes,
* projections,
* contracts,
* and identities.

The symbolic layer allows players to understand these ideas through experience before encountering their technical implementation.

---

# Relationship To The Commonwealth

The long-term vision is that successful ideas emerging from the symbolic world may eventually inform the evolution of the Pancakes Commonwealth.

This should occur cautiously.

Not every entertaining mechanic belongs in a real community.

Likewise, not every successful institution can be represented perfectly within a game.

The RPG serves as one source of evidence rather than the sole authority.

Its greatest value lies in helping people reason about systems together.

---

# The Scientific Method

The Pitchfork RPG encourages an iterative process.

Observe.

Imagine.

Design.

Build.

Play.

Measure.

Reflect.

Improve.

Repeat.

This cycle applies equally to magical research and institutional research.

The Academy teaches players to become investigators rather than merely followers of tradition.

---

# Success

The economic testbed succeeds when players begin asking better questions.

Not simply:

> How do I earn more resources?

But:

* Why does this institution work?
* How can this community cooperate more effectively?
* What assumptions produced this outcome?
* Can we invent something better?
* What have we overlooked?

At that point the symbolic world has fulfilled its purpose.

Players are no longer only mastering the game's systems.

They are learning how to reason about the systems that allow civilizations to flourish.

The ultimate goal is not to discover a perfect economy.

It is to cultivate communities capable of continuously improving their own institutions through observation, experimentation, and shared stewardship.
