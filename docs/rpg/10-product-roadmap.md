# Product Roadmap

## Introduction

The Pitchfork RPG is intended to evolve gradually from a simple single-player browser game into a family of interoperable clients built upon the Pitchfork platform.

Each phase validates both technical architecture and design assumptions before introducing additional complexity.

The roadmap emphasizes learning over rapid expansion.

Every stage should produce a complete, enjoyable product while laying the foundation for future work.

---

# Guiding Principles

The roadmap follows several principles.

* Build simple systems before complex systems.
* Validate ideas symbolically before deploying them in real communities.
* Prefer extensible foundations over feature accumulation.
* Build one coherent platform rather than disconnected products.
* Ensure every phase provides immediate value to users.
* Allow research findings to influence later phases.

The objective is not simply to ship software.

The objective is to develop a resilient ecosystem.

---

# Phase 1 — Core Browser RPG

## Goal

Build an enjoyable single-player role-playing game that introduces the symbolic world.

## Focus

Players should be able to:

* attend the Academy,
* explore the world,
* gather resources,
* learn color magic,
* practice alchemy,
* craft equipment,
* improve a sanctuary,
* complete quests,
* and progress through the Novice's Journey.

The game should stand on its own even without external integrations.

## Validation

Demonstrate that:

* the symbolic world is compelling,
* the magic system is understandable,
* crafting is enjoyable,
* exploration is rewarding,
* and the educational progression feels natural.

This phase validates the core player experience.

---

# Phase 2 — Pitchfork Integration

## Goal

Replace isolated game state with the Pitchfork platform.

## Focus

Introduce:

* event-based accounting,
* inventories,
* resources,
* recipes,
* contracts,
* identities,
* and projections.

Gameplay should remain familiar while becoming powered by the underlying platform.

## Validation

Demonstrate that a symbolic game can operate naturally on top of the Pitchfork architecture.

---

# Phase 3 — Real-World Participation

## Goal

Connect symbolic gameplay to everyday life.

## Focus

Allow selected real-world activities to contribute symbolic resources.

Examples include:

* walking,
* learning,
* cooking,
* gardening,
* journaling,
* household stewardship,
* creativity,
* caregiving,
* and community participation.

The emphasis remains on symbolic interpretation rather than gamification.

## Validation

Determine whether real participation enriches the game without making it feel like a productivity application.

---

# Phase 4 — Shared Civilization

## Goal

Introduce persistent multiplayer institutions.

## Focus

Players begin forming:

* guilds,
* research groups,
* sanctuaries,
* workshops,
* marketplaces,
* libraries,
* academies,
* and councils.

Communities become long-lived participants in the world.

The emphasis shifts from individual progression toward collective achievement.

## Validation

Evaluate cooperation, institutional growth, and long-term stewardship.

---

# Phase 5 — Contract Magic

## Goal

Expand the world through player-created agreements.

## Focus

Introduce:

* player-created contracts,
* commissions,
* apprenticeships,
* collaborative projects,
* guild governance,
* educational contracts,
* and institutional responsibilities.

Players increasingly organize the world themselves.

## Validation

Determine whether contract systems encourage healthy cooperation and meaningful gameplay.

---

# Phase 6 — The Economic Testbed

## Goal

Enable structured experimentation with symbolic economic systems.

## Focus

Communities begin exploring:

* alternative resource systems,
* governance models,
* institutional structures,
* stewardship mechanisms,
* incentive systems,
* symbolic currencies,
* and cooperative organizations.

Research becomes a first-class gameplay activity.

## Validation

Evaluate institutional behavior rather than simply player progression.

---

# Phase 7 — Research Community

## Goal

Support community-driven extension of the platform.

## Focus

Provide tools for:

* creating magical schools,
* designing rituals,
* authoring quests,
* defining resources,
* extending recipes,
* proposing governance models,
* documenting discoveries,
* and conducting collaborative research.

The boundary between players and contributors begins to disappear.

## Validation

Determine whether the community can meaningfully extend the symbolic world.

---

# Phase 8 — Multiple Clients

## Goal

Demonstrate that the Pitchfork platform supports many interpretations.

## Possible Clients

Examples include:

* browser RPG,
* mobile companion,
* wellness applications,
* household management,
* educational clients,
* ambient simulation,
* ecological games,
* creative tools,
* and future experimental interfaces.

Each client shares the same underlying platform while expressing it differently.

## Validation

Confirm that the underlying architecture supports multiple independent user experiences.

---

# Phase 9 — Persistent Commonwealth

## Goal

Support long-lived communities built around shared stewardship.

## Focus

Players establish:

* academies,
* commonwealths,
* research institutions,
* educational programs,
* cultural traditions,
* regional governance,
* and inter-community cooperation.

Civilization becomes the primary long-term gameplay system.

The world continues evolving across generations of players.

## Validation

Observe whether institutions remain resilient over long periods of time.

---

# Future Directions

Many possibilities remain intentionally open.

Examples include:

* procedural world generation,
* artificial intelligence mentors,
* advanced world simulation,
* ecological modeling,
* symbolic scientific research,
* educational curricula,
* community governance tooling,
* augmented reality,
* additional game genres,
* and integrations with future Pancakes clients.

These ideas should emerge naturally from the evolving needs of the community rather than being implemented according to a fixed schedule.

---

# Relationship To The Pitchfork Platform

The RPG should never become a special case.

Every major system introduced by the game should correspond to reusable capabilities within the underlying Pitchfork platform.

The browser RPG is therefore both:

* a complete product,
* and a reference implementation demonstrating what the platform can support.

Future clients should be able to reuse the same accounting, contracts, resources, recipes, identities, and event model without inheriting fantasy-specific assumptions.

---

# Relationship To The Commonwealth

The roadmap ultimately extends beyond software.

As the symbolic world matures, players gain opportunities to explore increasingly sophisticated questions about cooperation, governance, stewardship, and civilization.

Ideas developed inside the game may inform future Commonwealth institutions.

Likewise, experience from real communities may inspire new symbolic mechanics.

The relationship remains iterative rather than one-directional.

---

# Success

The roadmap succeeds when each phase leaves the project stronger than before.

A successful phase does not merely add features.

It validates assumptions, simplifies future work, and expands the community's ability to participate.

The final destination is not simply a massively multiplayer role-playing game.

It is a family of interoperable Pitchfork clients connected by a shared symbolic language, a common architectural foundation, and a community that continuously improves both the fictional civilization and the real-world ideas it was designed to explore.
