# Symbolic Materials

## Introduction

Materials are the physical vocabulary of the magical world.

Every spell, ritual, artifact, sanctuary, and institution ultimately depends upon materials gathered from the world and transformed through symbolic crafting.

To a novice, materials appear to be ordinary physical objects.

To experienced practitioners, every material carries layers of symbolic meaning that influence how it participates in magic.

Materials are therefore more than resources.

They are carriers of possibility.

---

# Materials And Meaning

A material does not possess a single magical property.

Instead, it possesses a collection of symbolic associations that may become important in different contexts.

For example, silver might participate in recipes involving:

* purification,
* healing,
* reflection,
* the moon,
* mirrors,
* protection,
* conductivity,
* exchange,
* craftsmanship,
* or memory.

None of these meanings is uniquely correct.

The meaning emerges from the recipe, ritual, institution, or culture using the material.

Magic is interpretive.

---

# Symbolic Affordances

Every material possesses symbolic affordances.

These describe the kinds of transformations for which a material is naturally suited.

Affordances are not rigid rules.

They are invitations.

Two different magical traditions may legitimately discover different uses for the same material.

This allows magical knowledge to continue evolving across generations.

---

# Provenance

A material carries its history.

Where it came from matters.

Who gathered it matters.

Who refined it matters.

Who gifted it matters.

How it was transported matters.

Two pieces of silver may appear identical while possessing entirely different symbolic histories.

Provenance therefore becomes part of the material itself.

---

# Transformation

Materials rarely remain in their original form.

Through symbolic crafting they become:

* tools,
* relics,
* contracts,
* books,
* sanctuaries,
* artworks,
* rituals,
* magical devices,
* and institutions.

The value of civilization comes not only from gathering resources but from transforming them wisely.

---

# Recipes

Recipes describe transformations.

A recipe specifies:

* required materials,
* symbolic relationships,
* preparation,
* sequence,
* conditions,
* participants,
* and expected outcomes.

Recipes preserve knowledge across generations.

Civilization advances because recipes accumulate.

---

# Ecology

Materials originate within living ecosystems.

Gathering therefore carries responsibilities.

Players are encouraged to consider:

* sustainability,
* restoration,
* biodiversity,
* seasonal cycles,
* habitat,
* and long-term stewardship.

The magical world should become healthier through careful participation rather than continuous extraction.

---

# Real-World Correspondence

The symbolic materials of the RPG intentionally resemble real materials.

Players already understand many of their physical properties.

The magical world extends those understandings through symbolism rather than replacing them.

This creates a bridge between:

physical understanding,

cultural history,

symbolic interpretation,

and magical practice.

---

# Symbolic Frequencies

Different cultures often associate materials with recurring symbolic themes.

Examples might include:

* healing,
* protection,
* abundance,
* remembrance,
* hospitality,
* wisdom,
* courage,
* purification,
* craftsmanship,
* and celebration.

These recurring associations are referred to as symbolic frequencies.

Symbolic frequencies are descriptive rather than prescriptive.

They emerge from history, culture, and repeated practice rather than being assigned by universal law.

As the community grows, symbolic frequencies should evolve through collective discovery.

# Civilization As Material Transformation

One of the deepest lessons of the Academy is that civilization itself is built from materials.

Homes.

Libraries.

Bridges.

Workshops.

Gardens.

Guild halls.

Observatories.

Books.

Tools.

Everything begins as gathered materials and becomes something greater through knowledge, cooperation, and craftsmanship.

The prosperity of a civilization therefore depends not merely upon what resources it possesses, but upon how wisely it transforms them.

---

# Design Philosophy

Symbolic materials should never become a rigid catalogue assigning one magical effect to one commodity.

Instead, they provide a shared vocabulary from which recipes, rituals, institutions, and cultures construct meaning.

A single material may participate in countless magical traditions.

Likewise, the same symbolic idea may be expressed through many different materials.

This flexibility encourages experimentation, cultural diversity, and continued discovery.

The Academy teaches the current understanding of materials.

Future generations are expected to uncover new relationships, develop new recipes, and discover entirely new ways in which the physical world participates in magic.
